## Voice

Warm, curious, and peer-to-peer — like a knowledgeable colleague who makes complex ideas feel approachable by reaching for the right everyday analogy. The tone is casual and colloquial from the first sentence, never stiff or expert-to-beginner.
**Address:** Second person "you/your" throughout. Solo-authored voice, though a collaborative spirit comes through in tone.
**Rhythm:** Conversational sentences that build toward a relatable payoff. Fragments are welcome for emphasis. Complex concepts get grounded in concrete, familiar comparisons before technical detail is introduced.

------

## Opening patterns

Lead with a relatable concept, scenario, or reframe that bridges the familiar to the new. The opening establishes a mental model — usually through analogy — before introducing the Salesforce-specific layer.



> *"Cloud computing changed this concept by offering services to many companies at the same time."*

The signature rhythm: start broad, zoom in, land on the Salesforce angle with a concrete image.
What to avoid:

- Post-announcing openers ("In this post, I'll explain...")
- Expert-to-beginner framing ("As an admin, you need to know...")
- Abstract definitions before grounding the reader in something familiar

------

## Recurring phrases

No fixed signature phrases — the voice relies on casual, colloquial register rather than repeated markers. Write as if talking to a fellow admin, not presenting to an audience.
Use conversational connectors naturally:

- "Here's the thing..."
- "Try this..."
- "The good news is..."
- "Think of it like..."

Never force these — if a sentence works without them, leave it out.

------

## Structural devices

- **Analogy-first explanation:** Introduce a concept through a familiar real-world parallel (houses, apartment buildings, pair programming) before layering in the Salesforce specifics. The analogy should do real explanatory work, not just decorate.
- **Concrete scenario enumeration:** When illustrating variety or range, list short, vivid examples ("a cat-friendly home, another might have an impressive kitchen..."). Keep them punchy — two to four is usually enough.
- **Contextual framing for new features:** Before describing what a feature does, orient the reader with why it exists or what problem it solves. ("Programmers should be familiar with the idea of pair programming...")
- **Pro Tip callouts:** Use sparingly to surface a quick shortcut or a gotcha the reader might miss. Not a replacement for inline explanation.
- **Use case lists:** When a feature has multiple applications, a short bulleted list of "what you can do with this" works well — especially for newer or evolving tools.

------

## Closing pattern

Close with a brief, plain-language summary of the key takeaway — one to two sentences that crystallize the "so what" — followed by a resource list. The prose CTA should point the reader toward action without being promotional.
Structure:

1. One or two sentences summarizing the core takeaway in practical terms
2. Resource list (Trailhead module first, then additional links)

Never close with a restatement of everything covered in the post. Never end with promotional language about Salesforce products.

## What this skill adjusts

This skill adjusts voice and tone. It does not override the Admin Blog standards. The following always apply:

- **Structure:** intro, 3–4 sections with descriptive headlines, conclusion — all follow Salesforce Admin Blog guidelines
- **Voice:** always "you/your" — never "Admins do/can/should"
- **Mechanics:** no em-dashes, no AI-sounding metaphors, no post-announcing openers, active voice
- **Publishing requirements:** minimum 1,000 words, Trailhead module in resources, concrete CTA
- **Anti-patterns:** no fluff openers, no promotional closing, no summarizing what was just said
- **Personal voice:** light personal touches are welcome where they add warmth or context — but personal anecdotes and opinions must serve the reader, not showcase the author. This is a teaching blog, not a lifestyle magazine.
